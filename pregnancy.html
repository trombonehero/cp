Exercise in Pregnancy

Exercise is an important component of staying healthy and comfortable during pregnancy. As your body experiences the many changes involved, it can be difficult to find the motivation or energy to take care of yourself, but determining to do even a little exercise each week can make a big difference to how you feel - both physically and emotionally. If you make the effort you will find the benefits to be well worth it. You may find relief from pain or discomfort, feel more connected with your body, a better sense of well-being, even an increase in overall energy! Everybody is different, so the type and amount of exercise you do should be particular to your needs and abilities. Talk to your doctor or midwife about what's best for you.

Prenatal (or Antenatal) Pilates

Pilates is often an excellent choice of exercise for before, during and after pregnancy. With low or no impact, it focusses on controlled movements strengthening those muscles most involved in pregnancy and childbirth, and mobilizing joints which can help to relieve pain and discomfort. Many women experience back pain during pregnancy; and the stabilizing, strengthening and mobilizing exercises of Pilates have been shown to be particularly effective in this area.

As with most exercise, the sooner you start, the better. Even if you are only thinking about becoming pregnant at this point, joining a Pilates class is a great way to begin preparing your body. Also, you can join any beginner level class at this stage, so your options are plentiful - but do make sure to tell your instructor as soon as you know you are pregnant. As you progress in your pregnancy, though, a regular class will become inappropriate for your body, so finding a class or instructor that offers Prenatal Pilates would be best.

If you are trying Pilates for the first time when you are already pregnant, you should find a class or instructor that offers Prenatal Pilates, especially if you are past your first trimester. Make sure to speak to your doctor before starting any new form of exercise; your instructor may require your doctor's consent before beginning your Pilates programme. You may wish to contact your instructor before booking classes or one-to-ones; this will give you both the opportunity to ask questions and discuss the options available to you.

As I am currently pregnant myself, I can relate to the many joys and concerns in this exciting time. Personally I find Pilates invaluable, now more than ever, as it helps me remain confident and connected to my body as I manage the many changes of pregnancy. I strongly recommend you to take the time to discover if Prenatal Pilates is right for you.
